DSCR loans in Nevada: the Vegas math nobody runs

Nevada offers no state income tax and modest property taxes — then complicates the picture with a two-tier tax cap, some of the strictest STR rules in the country, and HOA dues on nearly everything. Here's how it all hits your DSCR.

MA
Reviewed by Moh Alloo, Mortgage Loan Originator · NMLS #2732105 · West Capital Lending
Updated July 6, 2026

Why investors keep buying Nevada

Las Vegas has spent two decades absorbing California out-migration — households chasing lower costs and no state income tax — and the metro's economy has diversified well beyond the Strip: healthcare, logistics, pro sports, a growing tech footprint. That in-migration keeps the renter pool deep. It also attracted institutional capital early: large single-family rental operators own a meaningful slice of Vegas housing stock, which means you're competing with funds on acquisitions but also benefiting from the rent floor their professional management sets.

Up north, Reno–Sparks runs on a different engine: the Tahoe-Reno Industrial Center, warehousing, and advanced manufacturing payrolls that pulled in tens of thousands of jobs. Price points are higher than Vegas relative to rents, but tenant quality and wage growth are strong.

A DSCR loan — qualifying on the property's rent-to-payment ratio instead of your personal income — fits Nevada well, especially for the self-employed and California investors whose tax returns understate their real cash flow. But three Nevada-specific factors move the ratio: the property tax cap structure, STR licensing reality, and HOA dues.

No income tax, and a property tax system with a two-tier cap

Nevada has no state income tax on your rental profits — a permanent, structural edge over California next door. Property taxes are low-to-moderate, typically effective around 0.5–0.7% of market value, partly because Nevada assesses only 35% of taxable value and applies a depreciation allowance of 1.5% per year (up to 50 years) on the structure. Older homes literally get a tax discount for age.

The part investors miss is the abatement cap two-tier. Nevada caps how fast a property's tax bill can grow each year — but the cap depends on occupancy. Owner-occupied primary residences are capped at 3% annual increases. Rentals, second homes, and commercial property fall under the "high cap," which is set by formula each year and can run up to 8%. Two practical consequences. First, your rental's tax bill can compound nearly three times faster than the identical primary residence next door, so a ten-year hold sees real divergence — underwrite the trajectory, not just year one. Second, the county applies the cap based on a filed declaration; buy a former primary residence and fail to file correctly, and you can get a corrected bill with back charges. File the cap card at purchase and budget the 8% path.

The Vegas STR reality is harsher than the podcast said

Out-of-state buyers routinely assume Las Vegas is an Airbnb gold mine. The regulatory reality: Henderson effectively prohibits short-term rentals. The City of Las Vegas licenses them narrowly, with owner-occupancy requirements for some categories and 660-foot separation rules between licensed units. And unincorporated Clark County — where a huge share of "Las Vegas" addresses actually sit, including most of the area near the Strip — only began licensing under state mandate, and did it grudgingly: a limited application lottery, 660-foot minimum separation between STRs, a 2,500-foot buffer from resort hotels, occupancy limits, and fines that can reach into the tens of thousands for unlicensed operation. Enforcement is active.

The lending consequence is direct: no lender will underwrite short-term rental income from an unlicensed unit, and licenses are scarce by design. The realistic Vegas DSCR play is long-term or mid-term (30+ day) furnished rental income — the latter is genuinely strong here given traveling healthcare workers and relocating households. If you hold a legal STR license, great; bring the paperwork. If not, run your numbers on market rent.

HOAs everywhere — and dues hit your DSCR

Vegas housing stock is dominated by master-planned communities, so HOA dues show up on most deals — and dues are part of PITIA, the payment your rent must cover. Nevada also carries unique HOA history: under NRS 116, an HOA's lien for roughly nine months of dues has super-priority over the first mortgage, and a 2014 Nevada Supreme Court decision confirmed that an HOA foreclosure could wipe out the first deed of trust entirely. Lenders remember. Expect scrutiny of HOA delinquency status at closing, and on condos, a full questionnaire — projects with litigation or weak budgets can fall into non-warrantable territory.

Reno, Sparks, and the Tahoe edge

Washoe County runs the same two-tier tax cap structure. Reno licenses STRs in tiers with caps on occupancy and parking; the contentious zone is Incline Village and the Washoe Tahoe area, where STR permits are limited and neighbor enforcement is enthusiastic. The clean Reno–Sparks DSCR play is long-term rentals near the industrial corridor, where wage growth supports steady rent escalation. Insurance runs modestly higher near the wildland interface west of Reno — get a quote if the property backs to open hillside.

Getting a Nevada DSCR loan done

Nevada closes fast — escrow-state mechanics, investor-familiar title companies, and routine LLC vesting on business-purpose loans. Bring the HOA resale package early (Nevada requires one, and it surfaces delinquency and litigation problems while you can still negotiate), file your tax cap declaration at closing, and underwrite on lease income unless you genuinely hold an STR license. Do that, and Nevada is one of the smoother states in the country to build a rental portfolio in.

Frequently asked questions

Can I get a DSCR loan for a short-term rental in Las Vegas?
Only if the property holds a valid license, and those are scarce. Henderson effectively bans STRs, the City of Las Vegas licenses narrowly with separation rules, and unincorporated Clark County uses a lottery with 660-foot spacing and a 2,500-foot resort buffer. Without a license, lenders will underwrite long-term market rent instead of nightly income.
How does Nevada's property tax cap work for rentals?
Nevada caps annual tax bill increases at 3% for owner-occupied primary residences but up to 8% for rentals and second homes. Your rental's bill can grow nearly three times faster than an identical primary residence. File the correct cap declaration when you buy, or the county can issue corrected bills with back charges.
Do HOA dues count against my DSCR?
Yes. HOA dues are part of PITIA, the full monthly payment your rent must cover. In Vegas's HOA-heavy housing stock, a $100-plus monthly assessment can be the difference between qualifying and missing at a given leverage. Compare listings on dues, not just price and rent.
Is there state income tax on Nevada rental income?
Nevada has no state income tax, so the state takes nothing from your rental profits. You still owe federal income tax, and if you live in another state such as California, your home state generally taxes that income too. The advantage is largest for investors who live in Nevada or other no-tax states.
Why do lenders care about the HOA's finances on a Nevada deal?
Nevada law gives HOA liens super-priority over the first mortgage for a period of unpaid dues, and Nevada courts confirmed an HOA foreclosure can extinguish the lender's deed of trust. Lenders respond by checking that dues are current and, on condos, reviewing the project's budget, delinquencies, and litigation before closing.
